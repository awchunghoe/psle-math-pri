What it is

Items come in identical groups/sets (e.g., 3 pens and 5 pencils per set). The problem gives a total difference between the two item types across all groups. Students find the per-group difference, then divide total difference by per-group difference to get the number of groups.

Signal phrases — train students to spot

  • "Each box contains 4 apples and 7 oranges"
  • "Every set has 3 red and 5 blue marbles"
  • "There were 24 more pencils than pens"
  • Identical groups with a total difference given

Question: A teacher bought some stationery sets. Each set contains 3 pens and 5 pencils. She bought 24 more pencils than pens. How many sets did she buy?

🔥 Common trap

Students add 3 + 5 = 8, then do 24 ÷ 8 = 3 sets. Wrong! The 24 is a difference, not a total. Train them to ask: "Is the given number a total or a difference?"

🌱 Teaching tip

Draw exactly one set first and label the per-set difference. Then ask: "If one set gives me 2 extra pencils, how many sets give me 24 extra?" This "unitising" approach builds intuition before the division step.

💡 Show full mark scheme (3 marks)

M1: Find per-set difference: 5 - 3 = 2

M1: Divide total difference by per-set difference: 24 ÷ 2 = 12

A1: 12 sets
