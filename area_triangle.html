What it is

When two or more triangles share the same height (usually because they sit between the same parallel lines), their areas are in the same ratio as their bases. Students must spot the shared dimension and use the area formula, not guess.

Signal phrases — train students to spot

  • "Triangles ABC and ABD share the same height"
  • "The triangles are between the same parallel lines"
  • "Find the area of the shaded triangle" (often one of a pair)
  • "Given area of triangle PQR, find area of triangle PQS"

Common student traps

⚠️ Trap 1: Assuming same base means same area

Students see triangles on the same line and assume equal areas. Only true if both base AND height are equal. Triangles on the same base but different heights have different areas.

⚠️ Trap 2: Forgetting the ½ in the area formula

Students write "8 × 10 = 80" instead of "½ × 8 × 10 = 40." The ½ is easy to drop when rushing. Emphasise writing the full formula every single time.

⚠️ Trap 3: Using the wrong segment as the base

In △ PQS, students sometimes use QR or SR as the base instead of QS. The base must be the side that sits on the shared line with the known measurement.

How to teach it

💡 Three teaching anchors

1. Trace the height with a bright colour. Physically draw the perpendicular dotted line from P to the base. Students often can't "see" the height until it's highlighted.

2. Write the full formula every time. Area = ½ × base × height. Don't let students skip to arithmetic. The formula step catches base/height mix-ups.

3. Teach the shortcut AFTER the method. "Same height → area ratio = base ratio" is a powerful shortcut, but only after students can derive it from the formula. Otherwise it becomes a guess.
