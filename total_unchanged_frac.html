What it is

Money (or items) moves from one person to another — but the total amount stays the same. Students draw a combined "total" bar, mark the transfer, and use the new ratio to find what each person had.

Signal phrases — train students to spot

  • "Ali gave Baba $40. The ratio then became 3:2"
  • "Some money was transferred from A to B" — total is the same
  • "In the end, A had 3 times as much as B" after a transfer
  • Look for the transfer amount as a concrete number

Question: Ali and Baba had $120 altogether. After Ali gave Baba $16, Ali had 3 times as much as Baba. How much did Ali have at first?

Common student traps

⚠️ Trap 1: Forgetting total is unchanged

Students try to add $16 to the total or subtract it. The $16 just moved — total stays $120. This is the key insight.

⚠️ Trap 2: Wrong direction on the $16

To find Ali's original, students subtract $16 instead of adding. Ali gave away $16, so he had MORE before. Give = add back.

⚠️ Trap 3: Ratio total ≠ number of people

3:1 means 4 parts total, not 2 parts. Students sometimes divide $120 by 2 or 3 instead of 4.

How to teach it

💡 Three teaching anchors

1. Draw ONE combined bar for the total. Don't draw separate bars for Ali and Baba initially. One bar = $120, split by the ratio.

2. Mark the transfer with an arrow. Physically draw $16 moving from Ali to Baba. Students need to see it as movement, not magic.

3. "Give back" rule. To undo a transfer, go the opposite direction. Ali gave → to find before, Ali receives back.
