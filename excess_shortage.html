What it is

Items are distributed equally among a group. Under one plan, there are items left over (excess). Under another plan, there are not enough (shortage). The total difference between the two plans reveals how many recipients there are. Students compare the two scenarios and divide the total gap by the per-person difference.

Signal phrases — train students to spot

  • "If each pupil gets 3 sweets, there are 10 left over"
  • "If each pupil gets 4 sweets, there is a shortage of 5"
  • "When packed in bags of 6, 8 remain. When packed in bags of 7, 2 more needed"
  • Two scenarios with different per-group amounts and different surplus/deficit

Question: A teacher has some sweets for her class. If each pupil gets 3 sweets, there will be 10 sweets left over. If each pupil gets 4 sweets, there will be a shortage of 5 sweets. How many pupils are there in the class?

🔥 Common trap

Students subtract the two surpluses: 10 - 5 = 5 pupils. Wrong! When one scenario has excess and the other has shortage, you must ADD them: 10 + 5 = 15. The shortage represents sweets that would be needed ON TOP OF the excess — they move in opposite directions.

🌱 Teaching tip

Draw both scenarios stacked vertically. Ask: "How many more sweets do I need to give 4 instead of 3 to each person?" The answer is 1 per person. "Where do I get them from?" First use the 10 extras, then I still need 5 more. So total needed = 10 + 5 = 15, which equals 1 per person × 15 people.

M1: Correct method: (10 + 5) ÷ (4 - 3) = 15 (or equivalent reasoning)

M1: Total sweets = 55 (from either plan)

A1: 15 pupils
