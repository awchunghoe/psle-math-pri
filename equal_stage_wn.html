What it is

Two people start with different amounts. After one gives some to the other, they end up equal. Students draw before-and-after bars and use the "gap closed" to find the transfer amount and original values.

Signal phrases — train students to spot

  • "After Ali gave Baba $30, they had the same amount"
  • "In the end, both of them had equal amounts of money"
  • "A transfer happened, and the two quantities became equal"
  • The initial ratio is given (e.g., 3 times as much)

Common student traps

⚠️ Trap 1: Thinking the transfer = the gap

The gap is 2 units ($60), but Ali only gives $30. Students think "Ali gives everything extra." No — Ali gives half the gap so both end up equal.

⚠️ Trap 2: Adding $30 to the total

Students add $30 to the total thinking new money appeared. The $30 already existed — it just moved. Total before = total after.

⚠️ Trap 3: Setting 3u = $30

Students see "gave $30" and think that's the unit value. But $30 is only 1 unit (half the gap), not 3 units.

How to teach it

💡 Three teaching anchors

1. "Close the gap." The gap between Ali and Baba must be split in half. One half stays with Ali, the other goes to Baba. This is the $30.

2. Draw the after bars as equal length. Physically redraw both bars the same length after step 4. The visual equality makes the algebra obvious.

3. Check with the total. Before total ($90 + $30 = $120) must equal after total ($60 + $60 = $120). This catches direction errors.

Try this one (no peeking)

Practice: Tom had 5 times as many stickers as Jerry. After Tom gave Jerry 24 stickers, they had the same number. How many stickers did Tom have at first?
▸ Click to reveal answer

Tom = 5u, Jerry = 1u. Gap = 4u.

Tom gives half the gap = 2u = 24 stickers → 1u = 12

Tom at first = 5 × 12 = 60 stickers

Check: 60 − 24 = 36, 12 + 24 = 36 ✓
