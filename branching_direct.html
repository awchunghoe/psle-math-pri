What it is

A problem where the starting amount is known, and fractions are applied sequentially — often to the remainder, not the original. Students must track the "active whole" at each stage: what looks like 1/4 is 1/4 of the remaining amount, not 1/4 of the starting total.

Signal phrases — train students to spot

  • "1/3 of them are red, 1/4 of the remainder are green, the rest are yellow"
  • "He spent 2/5 on food, then 1/2 of what was left on transport"
  • The starting amount is given; fractions are applied step-by-step
  • Each fraction is applied to the remainder, not the original whole

Common student traps

⚠️ Trap 1: Applying every fraction to the original

Students compute 1/4 × 120 = 30 (wrong) instead of 1/4 × 80 = 20 (right). They miss that "of the remainder" shifts the active whole from 120 down to 80.

⚠️ Trap 2: Adding fractions directly

Students try 1/3 + 1/4 = 7/12 and subtract from 1. This only works if both fractions share the same whole — they don't. 1/3 is of 120; 1/4 is of 80.

⚠️ Trap 3: Forgetting what the question asks

The question asks for yellow, but students stop at green (20) or red (40) and box that as the answer. Train them to re-read the last line before writing the final answer.

How to teach it

💡 Three teaching anchors

1. Underline "of the remainder" every time. This phrase is the trigger that tells students the active whole has changed. If it's missing, the fraction applies to the original.

2. Draw the zoomed bar physically separated. Don't just mentally re-cut the original bar — draw a fresh bar for the remainder so students see the new "whole" they're cutting.

3. Label units inside every box. Every section of the bar should have a number (or a ?) inside it. If a section is empty, the student has skipped a step.

Practice: A baker made 180 cakes. 2/5 were chocolate, 1/3 of the remainder were vanilla, and the rest were strawberry. How many strawberry cakes were there?
▸ Click to reveal answer

Chocolate: 2/5 × 180 = 72 cakes

Remainder: 180 − 72 = 108 cakes

Vanilla: 1/3 × 108 = 36 cakes

Strawberry: 108 − 36 = 72 cakes (or 3/4 × 108 = 72)

Check: 72 + 36 + 72 = 180 ✓
