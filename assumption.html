What it is

The problem has two types of items with a total count and a total value (e.g., total creatures and total legs). Instead of guess-and-check randomly, students assume all are one type, calculate the total, find the difference from the target, then swap in the other type to fix the gap. Each swap changes the total by a predictable amount.

Signal phrases — train students to spot

  • "There are 10 creatures with 34 legs altogether"
  • "Some are chickens (2 legs) and some are cows (4 legs)"
  • "A total of 20 vehicles with 64 wheels" — cars (4 wheels) and motorcycles (2 wheels)
  • Two unknowns with two totals given (count + value)

Question: A farmer has 10 chickens and cows altogether. They have 28 legs in total. How many cows are there? (Chickens have 2 legs, cows have 4 legs)

🔥 Common trap

Students guess randomly without a system, trying 5 cows, 3 cows, 7 cows... and get lost. They also forget that changing one cow to one chicken changes the total creature count too. The assumption method fixes this: total creatures stays at 10, only the type mix changes.

🌱 Teaching tip

Start with the assumption method (more efficient), then show the table as a backup. For the assumption: always assume the smaller-value item (chickens, not cows) — this gives a deficit that you close by upgrading. "Every time I swap a chicken for a cow, I gain 2 legs."

M1: Assume all chickens: 10 × 2 = 20 legs

M1: Difference = 8, per swap = 2, so cows = 8 ÷ 2 = 4
