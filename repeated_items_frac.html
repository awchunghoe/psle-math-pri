What it is

Items come in identical groups, but the problem specifies a fraction of each item type (e.g., "1/2 of the cars were red"). Students first calculate the actual number per group for the sub-type, find the per-group difference, then divide the total difference by the per-group difference.

Signal phrases — train students to spot

  • "Each set had 6 cars and 4 lorries. 1/2 of the cars..."
  • "In every box, 2/5 were red and the rest were blue"
  • "There were 16 more red cars than red lorries"
  • Fraction of a sub-type + total difference of that sub-type

Worked example — click through each step

Question: A factory made toy cars and lorries in sets. Each set had 6 cars and 4 lorries. 1/2 of the cars and 1/4 of the lorries were red. There were 16 more red cars than red lorries. How many sets were made?

🔥 Common trap

Students find the per-set difference of the total items (6 - 4 = 2) instead of the per-set difference of the red sub-type (3 - 1 = 2). Coincidentally the same number here — but the method is wrong! Train them to apply the fraction before finding the difference.

🌱 Teaching tip

Label the "red" sub-type clearly with a different colour (chocolate/red fill). The two-step structure — (1) find red per set, (2) find number of sets — is the key pattern. Have students verbalise: "First I find how many red per set, then I treat it like a normal Repeated Items problem."

💡 Show full mark scheme (4 marks)

M1: Red cars per set = 1/2 × 6 = 3

M1: Red lorries per set = 1/4 × 4 = 1

M1: Per-set difference = 2. Sets = 16 ÷ 2 = 8

A1: 8 sets
