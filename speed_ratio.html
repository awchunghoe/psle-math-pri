What it is

When two objects travel the same distance, their speeds and times are inversely proportional. Faster = less time. Students set up a ratio table: Speed A : Speed B = Time B : Time A (swapped).

Signal phrases — train students to spot

  • "Ali and Baba both travelled from X to Y" — same distance
  • "Ali took 3 hours, Baba took 2 hours" — times given
  • "Find the ratio of their speeds"
  • "If Ali's speed was 40 km/h, find Baba's speed"

Common student traps

⚠️ Trap 1: Same ratio instead of inverse

Students write Speed ratio = Time ratio = 4:6. Opposite! More time means less speed. Swap the ratio.

⚠️ Trap 2: Finding distance first (slow method)

Students compute D = 72 × 4 = 288, then do 288 ÷ 6 = 48. Correct answer but slow. The inverse ratio table skips the distance step entirely.

⚠️ Trap 3: Not checking "same distance"

The inverse ratio only works when both travel the same distance. If distances differ, use the full S = D ÷ T formula for each.

How to teach it

💡 Three teaching anchors

1. "Same distance → swap." Write this on the board. Time ratio 4:6 becomes speed ratio 6:4. The mnemonic: S and T are neighbours in the alphabet, they swap.

2. Verify with distance. After finding the answer, always check: did both cover the same distance? This catches the "not same distance" error.

3. Draw the table every time. The two-row table (Time / Speed) makes the inverse relationship tangible. Don't skip the table and go straight to algebra.

Try this one (no peeking)

Practice: Cindy and Dawn both ran the same route. Cindy took 5 minutes and Dawn took 8 minutes. If Cindy's speed was 120 m/min, what was Dawn's speed?
▸ Click to reveal answer

Time ratio 5:8 → Speed ratio 8:5 (inverse)

Cindy = 8 parts = 120 → 1 part = 15

Dawn = 5 × 15 = 75 m/min

Check: Cindy 120 × 5 = 600 m. Dawn 75 × 8 = 600 m ✓
