What it is

Problems involving rectangular tanks being filled, drained, or transferred between containers. Students draw a cross-section (side view) of the tank, mark the water level, and use Volume = length × width × height with careful unit conversion.

Signal phrases — train students to spot

  • "A rectangular tank measures 30 cm by 20 cm by 25 cm"
  • "Water is poured into / drained from the tank"
  • "Find the new water level" or "height of the water"
  • "The tank is filled to the brim" or "2/3 full"

Common student traps

⚠️ Trap 1: Forgetting to convert litres to cm³

Tank dimensions are in cm but volume is given in litres. Students plug 4.5 straight into cm formulas. Always convert first: 1 L = 1000 cm³.

⚠️ Trap 2: Using tank height instead of water height

The tank is 30 cm tall but water is only at 18 cm. Students calculate volume using 30 cm. Read the question — it says "filled to a height of 18 cm."

⚠️ Trap 3: Dividing volume by height instead of base area

Students do 4500 ÷ 18 = 250 (wrong). The correct formula: Volume = base area × height, so height = Volume ÷ base area = 4500 ÷ (40 × 25).

How to teach it

💡 Three teaching anchors

1. Draw the cross-section first. Side view with water shaded. Students need to SEE the water level, not just imagine it.

2. Write the conversion as step 1. 1 L = 1000 cm³. Write it at the top of every liquid volume question before touching any other numbers.

3. Label "water" vs "tank" clearly. Water height and tank height are different. Write "water = 18 cm" and "tank = 30 cm" as separate labels.

Try this one (no peeking)

Practice: A rectangular tank measures 50 cm × 30 cm × 40 cm. It contains water to a height of 24 cm. How many litres of water must be added to fill it to the brim?
▸ Click to reveal answer

Height needed = 40 − 24 = 16 cm

Volume = 50 × 30 × 16 = 24000 cm³

= 24000 ÷ 1000 = 24 litres
