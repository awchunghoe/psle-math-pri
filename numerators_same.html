What it is

A faster way to compare or arrange fractions when the LCM of the numerators is small. Instead of finding a common denominator (which can be huge), make the numerators identical. Then just compare denominators — with the same numerator, the fraction with the smaller denominator is larger.

Signal phrases — train students to spot

  • "Arrange 2/3, 4/7, 6/11 in ascending order" — numerators are simple multiples
  • "Which is the smallest: 3/5 or 6/11?" — one numerator is a multiple of the other
  • The LCM of numerators is smaller than the LCM of denominators
  • Usually appears in MCQ or short-answer ordering questions

Common student traps

⚠️ Trap 1: Same direction as common denominator

Students learn "common denominator → smaller numerator is smaller." They apply the same logic to common numerator: "smaller denominator is smaller." Opposite! With common numerator, smaller denominator means bigger fraction.

⚠️ Trap 2: Forgetting to multiply both parts

Students write "2/3 = 8/3" (only multiplied the top). Every equivalent fraction must multiply both numerator and denominator by the same number.

⚠️ Trap 3: Blindly using common denominator anyway

LCM of 3, 7, 15 is 315. Students dutifully convert all three to /315, then mis-calculate the new numerators. Common numerator (LCM = 8) is dramatically faster — train the instinct to check numerators first.

How to teach it

💡 Three teaching anchors

1. Drill the "check numerators first" habit. Before any ordering question, students should scan the numerators. If one is a simple multiple of another, common numerator is probably faster.

2. Say the rule out loud every time. "Same numerator, opposite direction — smaller denominator, bigger fraction." The muscle memory prevents the direction trap.

3. Always verify with decimals. 2/3 ≈ 0.67, 4/7 ≈ 0.57, 8/15 ≈ 0.53. A quick decimal check catches ordering errors before they get boxed.
