What it is

"Boomerang" and "Rugby" shapes are composites of semicircles arranged around a rectangle or square. The trick: cut and paste curved segments to transform the shape into something simple (a rectangle, a triangle, or a single circle). Never compute each semicircle separately.

Signal phrases — train students to spot

  • "The figure is made up of a rectangle and semicircles"
  • "Find the area of the shaded region" in a circle composite
  • "The figure consists of quadrants and straight lines"
  • Look for matching curved segments that can be swapped

Common student traps

⚠️ Trap 1: Computing 4 separate semicircles

Students calculate each semicircle individually with ½ × π × r² four times. The shortcut: pair the matching semicircles into full circles.

⚠️ Trap 2: Using diameter as radius

Students use r = 14 instead of r = 7. The formula uses radius, not diameter. Halve the diameter first.

⚠️ Trap 3: Forgetting the rectangle

Students sum the circle areas but forget to add the central rectangle. The total area = rectangle + all curved parts.

How to teach it

💡 Three teaching anchors

1. "Pair the matching curves." Two semicircles with the same diameter = one full circle. This is the first thing to do before any area calculation.

2. Label radius on every arc. Write "r = 7" directly on the semicircle. Prevents the diameter-as-radius error.

3. Trace the outline with your finger. The full perimeter helps students see which parts are "inside" (rectangle) vs "outside" (semicircles).

Try this one (no peeking)

Practice: A rectangle measures 20 cm × 12 cm with semicircles on all four sides. Find the total area of the figure. (Take π = 3.14)
▸ Click to reveal answer

Two semis on 20-cm side → 1 circle r=10: 3.14 × 100 = 314 cm²

Two semis on 12-cm side → 1 circle r=6: 3.14 × 36 = 113.04 cm²

Rectangle: 20 × 12 = 240 cm²

Total = 240 + 314 + 113.04 = 667.04 cm²
