What it is

Two quantities are compared. One stays exactly the same throughout the problem; the other changes by a fraction. Students use the unchanged item as an anchor — draw it first, then build the changed item's bar around it.

Signal phrases — train students to spot

  • "Baba had $24. After Ali spent 1/3 of his money, Ali had 2 times as much as Baba"
  • "Jerry had 30 marbles. After Tom gave away 1/5, Tom had twice as many as Jerry"
  • One person's amount is stated as a number and never changes
  • The other person's amount is described as a multiple of the unchanged one

Common student traps

⚠️ Trap 1: Drawing Ali first

Students start with Ali's unknown bar and get stuck. Always draw the unchanged item first — it's the only known length, so it's the anchor.

⚠️ Trap 2: Treating "2 times" as a fraction

Students write "2 times = 2/1" and get confused. "2 times as much" is a multiplier, not a fraction. Ali after = 2 × $24 = $48.

⚠️ Trap 3: Using the spent amount as the unit

Students see "spent 1/3" and make 1 unit = the amount spent. But the $48 is what's left (2 units), not what was spent (1 unit). Read the comparison line carefully.

How to teach it

💡 Three teaching anchors

1. "Who didn't move?" Ask this first, every time. The character whose amount is stated as a fixed number and never changes — that's your first bar.

2. Draw the AFTER state before the BEFORE. Counter-intuitive but effective. The "after" relationship (2 times) gives you a concrete length. Then work backwards to the original.

3. Label the fraction on the bar. Write "2/3" directly on Ali's after bar. When students see 2 units = $48 next to "2/3", the unit value becomes obvious.
